01 · Start here

Windows and Linux,
without the jargon.

Both are operating systems — the software your computer runs before anything else opens. The difference is who owns them and how they're sold.

⊞

Windows

Built by Microsoft · Paid software

Windows is like renting a fully-furnished flat. Microsoft built it, decorates it, and charges rent for every computer that uses it. In return, almost every app works out of the box and your staff already know their way around.

  • + Familiar to almost everyone
  • + Works with every commercial app
  • − A fee per computer, per year
  • − Microsoft decides the rules
vs
🐧

Linux

Built by a community · Free to use

Linux is like free land with a free blueprint. Anyone can build a house on it. You don't pay rent. But you need skilled builders to put it up, and some of your furniture from the old flat may not fit the new rooms.

  • + No fee for the software itself
  • + You control your own data
  • + Already runs most of the world's servers
  • − Needs skilled people to install and run

So why does any of this matter for these 11 companies? Because if a government or a bank with tens of thousands of computers decides to move from the rented flat to the free land, someone has to physically do it — design the new setup, move files and apps across, retrain staff, fix what breaks. That work is what these 11 companies sell.

02 · The news in one minute

Is Windows really
being replaced?

Short answer

Partly true. Not everywhere. Not overnight.

Some governments have made the move official. Most big businesses will keep Windows on staff laptops and only switch their behind-the-scenes systems. Either way, there's real work to be handed out — which is what these companies go after.

01

France has told its ministries to move

About 2.5 million government computers are in scope. Each ministry has to submit a plan by autumn 2026.

02

Germany and Denmark are doing smaller versions

One German state is moving 30,000 government computers. Denmark has started a similar plan.

03

Windows 10 stopped getting free updates

As of October 2025, Windows 10 no longer gets security fixes. Every business running it has to decide: upgrade to Windows 11, pay extra for updates, or move to something else. A fraction of them will pick Linux — and that fraction is big in absolute numbers.

04

But most companies won't flip entirely

A lot of everyday business software (design tools, trading desks, some games) only runs on Windows. So the realistic picture is a mix: Linux for servers and some offices, Windows for the rest.

05 · The two foreign platforms

The Linux behind the Linux —
Red Hat and Canonical.

These two aren't Indian IT firms — they're the actual makers of the Linux software that the Indian firms sell, install, and support.

🎩

Red Hat

American · Owned by IBM since 2019

Makes the most widely used paid version of Linux — think of it as "Linux with a warranty and a phone number to call when things break". Big governments and banks prefer paid Linux because they want someone accountable. Red Hat doesn't sell to customers directly that much — partners like HCL and Wipro do the selling.

07 · Additional info

Other companies likely to
get pieces of the pie.

You asked about eleven companies. In reality, a contract this big would be split across many vendors. These names aren't on your list but would almost certainly share in the work. Good to know they exist when someone brings them up.

🇮🇳 India

Infosys

The same size as TCS and Wipro. Not in your list, but no migration this big happens without Infosys being invited to bid.

🇺🇸 USA (India-heavy)

Cognizant

A New Jersey-listed firm that effectively operates like an Indian IT company. Similar services, similar pricing.

🌍 Global

Accenture

Global consulting and IT giant. Governments often choose big consultancies for projects this large — Accenture is usually the first one on the list.

🇫🇷 France

Capgemini

French consulting and IT giant. Almost a guaranteed winner of a chunk of the French government migration — a home-advantage contract.

🇫🇷 France

Atos · Sopra Steria

Two more French IT firms. France prefers to give big public-sector contracts to European firms for security and data-sovereignty reasons.

🇺🇸 USA

IBM

Owns Red Hat. If the chosen software is Red Hat's Linux, IBM itself often runs the migration as the primary vendor.

🌍 Global

Deloitte, PwC, KPMG, EY

The "Big Four" consultancies. They usually don't do the hands-on technical work, but advise governments on which vendors to pick and manage the project.

🇩🇪 Germany

SUSE

A German company that makes its own version of Linux — the third-biggest paid Linux after Red Hat and Ubuntu. Home advantage for the German migration.

One more point worth keeping in mind. Even if a contract goes to a European firm (say Capgemini or Atos), they rarely do all the work themselves. They hire Indian IT firms as subcontractors. So TCS, HCL, Wipro and the others still end up doing a lot of the actual migration — just with someone else's name on the top of the invoice.

A few honest caveats

  • This is not investment advice. It's a simple research brief. Do not treat any ranking here as a buy, sell or hold suggestion.
  • Numbers move daily. Revenues, company values and staff counts are from public filings and reliable sources around April 2026. Verify before acting on them.
  • Politics can change the answer. European governments may prefer European firms. That caps how much of a migration Indian firms win directly.
